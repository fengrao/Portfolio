Robo Repair

"How to engage students with special needs while teaching them concepts of math? "

0 / Product Preview

Robo Repair is a math educational game, which aims to help kindergarten through second grade students to understand concepts of addition and subtraction, and allows players to practice through engaging gameplay experience - fnding deflects and fixing robots. This game was selected to present at the Digital Media and Learning Conference 2017.

  • Role

    Game Designer, UX Designer

  • Duration

    4 months

01 / Background

Client Requirements
Our client is the Intermediate Unit 1 Educational Campus (IU1) at East Franklin, located in Waynesburg, Pennsylvania. IU1 currrent serves students K-12 who have mental health needs and emotional support needs. During this project, our main point of contact was a math teacher, Tracey Ross, who hoped us develop tools that can supplement her math curriculum, helping students understand math concept, but also provide engagements for students.

Target Audience
We went to East Franklin school ran by IU1, interviewing with the principal of the school, and a math teacher of a classroom. We specifically target at a classroom with 12 students. We noticed that the students in the classroom all have spacial needs such as autism as well as intellectual disabilities. Though their actual grade levels varied from kindergarden to fifth grade, their academic grade levels were about kingdergarden to second grade, which are much lower than usual students. Students with different ages are in the same classroom is highly special for our project.

Challenge

How do we help kids with behaviour issues and intelluctual disabilities to learn basic math while providing them engagement?

04 / Design

×

Brainstorming

For each math operation, each team member wrote as many ideas as possible in five minutes in terms of mechancis, theme, characters and story.

×

Whiteboarding

After we choosing our favorite idea, a robot theme concept, we whiteboarded main gameplay loop, basic interactions, and described the player journey.

Paper Prototype

Before moving to digital prototype, we wanted to verify our concept is is appealing to children and also effective in terms of learning goal. We created three versions of paper prototypes, each one has a mechanism to fix robot using math addition. The first one was using traditional number representation, the second one was stack, using size to represent how larger the numerb is. The third version involved different shapes of plugs and outlet, representing number with shape and quantities.We playtested with kids in East Franklin school, observing how they reacted to the prototypes.

Paper Prototype Playtest

We created three paper prototypes testing an addition mechanic. The first prototype involved combining screws to fix the robot; the second used stacking blocks to create larger ones; and the third involved plugs with different shapes and numbers.

Final Screens

05 / Evaluation

We playtested with kids in East Franklin School every two weeks. Through playtesting, we identified the issues in our game design, such as engagement, effectiveness in learning goal, etc., and also usability problems. Besides, we observed them playing, finding out their needs in further, adding features to polish our games, such as adding robots' dialogue, teacher's voice,etc.

Our product is now available on App Store for iPad.
