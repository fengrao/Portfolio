Robo Repair

"How to engage students with special needs while teaching them concepts of math? "

0 / Product Preview

Robo Repair is a math educational game, which aims to help kindergarten through second grade students to understand concepts of addition and subtraction, and allows players to practice through engaging gameplay experience - fnding deflects and fixing robots. This game was selected to present at the Digital Media and Learning Conference 2017.

  • Role

    Game Designer, UX Designer

  • Duration

    4 months

01 / Background

Client Requirements

Our client is the Intermediate Unit 1 Educational Campus (IU1) at East Franklin, located in Waynesburg, Pennsylvania. IU1 currrent serves students K-12 who have mental health needs and emotional support needs. During this project, our client hoped us develop tools that can supplement her math curriculum, helping students understand math concept, but also provide engagementsfor students.

Target Audience

We went to East Franklin school ran by IU1, and noticed that the students in the classroom all have spacial needs such as autism as well as intellectual disabilities. Though their actual grade levels varied from kindergarden to fifth grade, their academic grade levels were about kingdergarden to second grade, which are much lower than usual students. Students with different ages are in the same classroom is highly special for our project.

Challenge

How do we help kids with behaviour issues and intelluctual disabilities to learn basic math while providing them engagement?

03 / Design

Brainstorming

For each math operation, each team member wrote as many ideas as possible in five minutes in terms of mechancis, theme, characters and story

Robot Theme

Since our research suggest that children are really into robots and trucks, because there is a truck repairing shop around the school, we decided to use robot as our game concept.

Defects Finding Mechanics

From the interview with Jesse Schell, we learned that children hate explicitly instructional games. However, kids are fascinated about novel things. To avoid being a boring instructional game, we introduced defects finding mechanics, a novel interaction to excite kids. Players will use a scanner to find out where the hidden defects are.

Robot Fixing Mechanics

We noticed children enjoy exploring and crafting in Minecraft, so we defined our gameplay mechanics as discovering deflects in robot and fixing them to bring them into life. In this way, we wanted to give children some sense of discovery, and also agency so that they feel they have impacts in the virtual world.

×
Whiteboarding

After we choosing this idea, we whiteboarded main gameplay loop, basic interactions, and described the player journey.

Paper Prototype

Before moving to digital prototype, we wanted to verify our concept is is appealing to children and also effective in terms of learning goal.

Testing Robot Theme

Since we were not sure whether kids liked robots theme, so we made paper prototype of robot to see how they respond to it

Test Effective Number Sense Learning

In our finds, there are three major ways to teach number sense: size, shape and quantity. We decided to make three versions of paper prototype to test which one is more effective to these kids in terms of learning number sense.

Test Engagement

We wanted to test how engaged kids are with our robot mechanics. We set up camera recording their playing our game. I also wrote down the notes and about whether there were interesting or frustrating moments through observing they body languages.

Paper Prototype Playtest

We created three paper prototypes testing an addition mechanic. The first prototype involved combining screws to fix the robot; the second used stacking blocks to create a larger block; and the third involved plugs with different shapes and numbers. It turned out that stacking and shapes were not effective to help kids with number sense. In comparision, kids were more sensitive to quantity. They will count the screws to help them calculate the result.

Implementation & Iterations

After we found the robot theme worked for those kids, we started implementing our game. We decided to focus on using quanlity to help kids understand number sense, since from previous playtest we found quantity worked better, in terms of giving kids a sense how large the number is. We first implemented a 3D version, but then realized that kids preferred 2D art style, and it was much easier to produce art assets considering our time frame. Our second version was based on crafting and producing new screws. However, through playtest we found kids got lost and are confused about UI. The third version was based on screw counting. Player would need to place the right battery to fix robot, based on the screws on the body. Given the teacher hope that kids can reflect and apply what they learned in the class to the game, we changed the UI from horizontal to vertical version, since vertical form was how they learned to do the calculation.

New Challenge: Carry-over

The biggest challenge that kids encountered was carry-over. From the research, we noticed that, for these kids, the hardest part of number sense was, the number in TEN's place is not that number, but the number times ten.

Digit in Ten's Place

First, we wanted kids to understand one is not one when it is on ten's place, but it is ten. We again leveraged quantity to help children understand this concept. The TEN's place scaffolder is represented as metal panel. When a player taps a metal panel in TEN's place, it will shows ten screws coming from it. Showing the quantity of digit in TEN's place, we wanted kids actually sense the weight of the number, quantify the number, rather than just viewing it as a symbol with no meaning.

Carry-over

Carry-over is a hard concept. It is hard for kids with intelluctual disabilities to imagine how it work, so we wanted to visualize the process of carry-over, making it concrete rather than an abstract concept. In the game, when there are ten scaffolders in ONE's place, the scaffolders will shake and invite a player to tap on them. After being tapped, those ten scaffolders will be wrapped into a metal panel, moving the TEN's place, adding itself to scaffolders in TEN's place.

New Feature: Data Tracking & Profile

Our client also wanted to keep track of how well students are doing, and how much they improve. We then added a feature that each kid can create their own profile, and teacher can view the performance of each kid.

Secret Timer

Since those kids have behavioural issues, a explicit timer will hurt their feelings, we decided to add a hidden timer to keep track of how much time a kid take to finish an exercise level.

Boss Fighting

Besides average performance, the teacher also wanted a timing test on kids to see how many exercises can a kid finish in one minute. Setting up a timer counting down could make kids stressed, we created a timer that counting up, so that kids don't worried about the timing. We presented the test as a boss fight after finishing three levels. On the one hand, it changed the game pace, making it interesting; on the other hand, it serves as a test to fulfill our client's requirement.

04 / Evaluation

We playtested with kids in East Franklin School every two weeks. Through playtesting, we identified the issues in our game design, such as engagement, effectiveness in learning goal, etc., and also usability problems. Besides, we observed them playing, finding out their needs in further, adding features to polish our games, such as adding robots' dialogue, teacher's voice,etc.

Our product is now available on App Store for iPad.
